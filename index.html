Simulating the Impact of Village-Based Advisors on Regenerative Agriculture

We applied the field data to a probabilistic Monte Carlo simulation model to estimate the causal effect and economic value of a Village-Based Advisor (VBA) program on the adoption of Regenerative Agriculture (RA) practices among smallholder farmers in Kenya over a 25-year horizon. We combined a theory-based causal structure with a probabilistic framework to quantify not only the expected value of the VBA program but also the confidence in and risks associated with that outcome. The full model in R can be found here https://github.com/CWWhitney/RA_and_VBA/blob/main/vba_model_kenya.R

Our model structure is based on a pre-defined Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) (shown above) that outlines the impact pathway. The core logic follows this causal chain: Intervention: The VBA program increases the density and training quality of advisors. Adoption: These advisors, moderated by initial farmer trust, increase the adoption rate of RA practices. Biophysical Outcome: Higher adoption leads to improved maize yields, which are also influenced by stochastic environmental factors like rainfall variability and drought. Economic Outcome: The change in yield, combined with maize prices and the costs of both the program and practice adoption, determines the final net economic benefit.

To account for uncertainty, we defined all key input parameters as probability distributions (e.g., normal, uniform, posnorm). These distributions were specified in an input CSV file, capturing the range of plausible values for each variable based on literature, expert opinion, and field data. Our model performs a Monte Carlo simulation, running thousands of iterations. In each iteration, a value for every input is randomly sampled from its respective distribution, ensuring that the full range of uncertainty is propagated through the model. All variable estimates can be found here https://github.com/CWWhitney/RA_and_VBA/blame/main/data/inputs_vba_kenya.csv

The model calculates outcomes for two scenarios simultaneously: Control Scenario: Simulates a future where RA adoption continues from a field-calibrated baseline at a slow, organic growth trend, without any VBA program. Treatment Scenario: Simulates a future with the VBA program, where adoption is boosted by a factor derived from field data (vba_boost), which is itself a function of VBA density, effectiveness, training quality, and farmer trust.

We made key variables like adoption rates and yields time-variant using the decisionSupport vv() function, which introduces random annual fluctuations around a defined trend (e.g., a 0.5% annual increase in baseline adoption).

We modeled the impact of drought as a stochastic event using the chance_event() function, assigning an annual probability of occurrence and a corresponding negative yield impact.

For both scenarios, annual cash flows were calculated as: Annual Net Benefit = (Yield Maize Price) - (RA Adoption Costs) - (VBA Program Costs) The VBA program costs were only applied in the treatment scenario. The comparative annual net benefit was derived by subtracting the control net benefit from the treatment net benefit. These annual streams of comparative net benefits were then discounted to their Net Present Value (NPV) using a social discount rate. The Return on Investment (ROI) was calculated as the NPV of the comparative benefits divided by the NPV of the VBA program costs.

The primary model outputs are distributions for the Comparative NPV and ROI. From these distributions, we report the mean or median expected value, but more importantly, we calculate the Probability of Positive NPVthe proportion of simulation iterations that resulted in a net positive return. This provides a robust measure of the investment risk. Additional outputs on adoption and yield effects provide validation for the models causal pathway.
